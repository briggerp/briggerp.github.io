Tennis Enthusiast

I love playing tennis blabla. Tennis is my greatest passion. I started playing when I was six years old and dreamt of one day winning Wimbledon just like my big idol Roger Federer. Although I never hit such heights, I did manage to be ranked among the best 400 players in Switzerland, play at the national championships at juniors level and win the Under 16s championships of Central Switzerland. I also trained professionally for four months at the JC Ferrero Equelite Sport Academy in Spain. To me, tennis shaped me more than just the way I hit my forehand. It has shaped many of my life values. In particular, tennis has taught me: 1. Leadership 2. Fairness and honesty 3. Practice makes better 4. Mental resilience and patience 5. How fun it is to swing at a ball 1. Leadership I have been playing interclub tennis for more than a decade. Interclub is a team competition where Swiss tennis clubs compete against each other across different leagues. After a group phase, the first teams of each group compete to promote to the higher league while the last teams fight against relegation. In each matchup, six singles and three doubles are played. Whichever team wins more matches wins the matchup. In contrast to normal tennis competitions where players compete individually and coaching is prohibited, in interclub one competes as a team and coaching from the sidelines is explicitly allowed, even encouraged. To facilitate the matchups, each team is assigned a captain. The role of the captain is to make sure that the team has enough players, to decide which player plays in which position of the lineup and to organize the matchup with the opposing team. Since one year, I have been captaining my interclub team. It has been journey, culminating with the promotion to first league, and learning experience. In particular, I learned how to communicate in and manage a team effectively. I had to ensure that the we were enough players for the matchups and had to decide who is not allowed to play when we were too many players. I learned to take decisions confidently. 2. Fairness and honesty At amateur level, tennis is played without referees. Therefore, each player decides whether the ball is in or out on their side. Especially at juniors level, kids use this freedom to cheat. Although this may help lead to victory, I firmly believe that stolen victory does not bring intrinsic joy. On the other hand, showing fairness on the court is admirable and exemplary. If the ball is on the line, it's on the line. Instead of cheating, you need to be honest to yourself and your opponent, and focus on the next point instead. Having such a mindset helps you excel in every day life. There are some matters that happen which you simply cannot change or influence. You can only choose how to react to them. In the long run, being fair and honest will be the more fruitful option. 3. Practice makes better Tennis is a very technical sport. It takes many hours of practice to perfect a forehand or consistently hit a serve into the court. Repetition is unavoidable, serving a baskets of balls for half an hour straight the best way to feel comfortable with your serving motion. After my military service, I dreamt of playing college tennis in the US. Therefore, I moved to Spain and trained professionally at the JC Ferrero Equelite Sport Academy. Every day, we had three-and-a-half hours of tennis training and two hours of physical conditioning. For me, I was living a dream. What I learned is that being a professional athlete takes a lot of commitment, discipline and hard work. I also learned that even the best players in the world are not perfect and looking for ways how to get better. They still spend countless hours trying to improve their shots. To sum up, practice doesn't make perfect, it does however make better. And if done in the right way, it is lots of fun. 4. Mental resilience and patience In addition to being very technical, tennis is also a mental sport. Many matches are not won because a player has better shots. Instead, they are won because one player has a cooler head in the deciding moments. Therefore, it is very important to stay positive during bad phases of a match and to live a mindset of "this point matters and nothing else". These experiences help me to stay optimistic and have a clear mind when I experience stressful situations in every day life. 5. Tennis is fun - Taught me: leadership, honesty/good sport fairness, to work hard and train many hours , to be humble, My tennis mantra: "Enter and leave the court with a smile. Did you win? Then smile, you did everything right today. Did you lose? Then smile, your opponent showed you what you can improve on."
